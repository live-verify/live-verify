# The Doorstep Credential Stack

## The New Norm

Someone arrives at your door. They might be a plumber you booked last week, or a stranger in a high-vis jacket claiming to be from the water board. Either way, the check is the same: they present their credentials, you scan them, you see green and a matching face. The difference is in *who initiated the visit* — and that changes the dynamic, the stack, and the stakes.

This is the future norm. Not one credential checked reluctantly after a bad experience, but a **stack** of credentials checked routinely as a matter of course. Like a seatbelt — you don't decide whether to wear it based on the weather. You just do it. Every time.

## Two Doorsteps, Same Ritual

**The arranged visit:** You booked a Gas Safe engineer to replace your boiler. You know they're coming. You probably found them through a recommendation, a comparison site, or a trade body directory. The trust level is higher before they arrive — but the check still happens. The credential stack confirms what you already expected, and catches the edge cases (the company is registered but sent an unqualified labourer; the engineer lost a category since you last used them; their insurance lapsed last month).

**The cold caller:** Someone knocks. They say they're from the council, the water company, a charity, a roofing firm that "noticed your tiles are loose." You didn't invite them. You don't know them. The trust level is zero. The credential stack isn't confirming an expectation — it's the *only* basis for deciding whether to engage at all.

The ritual is identical in both cases: cards out, scan, photo match, proceed. But the meaning is different. For the arranged visit, checking is due diligence. For the cold caller, checking is the front door's immune system.

**The key insight:** if you only check cold callers, you're making a judgement about who "looks trustworthy" and who doesn't. That's the wrong instinct. The plumber you've used for years could have lost their Gas Safe registration last month. The smart cold caller with a fake lanyard and a clipboard is exactly the one who *looks* legitimate. Check everyone. Every time. The credential stack works precisely because it's indiscriminate.

## What's in the Stack

The stack varies by who's at the door and why.

### Arranged tradesperson

A typical tradesperson working on a domestic gas boiler replacement might carry:

| Credential | Issuer Domain | What It Proves |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Gas Safe ID Card | `gassaferegister.co.uk` | Qualified to work on gas — with specific appliance categories |
| Waste Carrier Licence | `environment-agency.gov.uk` | Authorised to take the old boiler away legally |
| Trader Tax Status | `hmrc.gov.uk` | VAT-registered (or legitimately below threshold) |
| Public Liability Insurance | `aviva.co.uk` (or insurer) | Covered if they damage your property |
| TrustMark Registration | `trustmark.org.uk` | Government-endorsed quality scheme |
| Employer ID | `coopergas.co.uk` | Current employee of the company they claim to work for |

An electrician's stack might include NICEIC instead of Gas Safe, plus an EV charger certification if they're installing a home charger. A roofer might carry a CSCS (Construction Skills Certification Scheme) card, a scaffolding competence card, and their contractor licence.

### Cold caller

A cold caller's stack is different — it answers "who are you and why are you here?" before any commercial discussion:

| Credential | Issuer Domain | What It Proves |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Employer ID | `thameswater.co.uk` | Current employee of the company they claim to represent |
| DBS Certificate (if applicable) | `gov.uk/dbs` | Background-checked for roles involving home access |
| Trading Standards Approved | `primaryauthority.beis.gov.uk` | Operating under a Primary Authority partnership |
| Charity Collection Permit | `hackney.gov.uk` (or local authority) | Licensed to collect door-to-door in this area |

The cold caller's stack is heavier on *identity and authority* (who are you, who sent you, are you allowed to be here) and lighter on *trade qualifications* (what can you do). The arranged tradesperson has already passed the "who are you" test by being booked — their stack is about *competence and compliance*.

For cold callers, the **photo return is critical**. An arranged tradesperson is expected — even without verification, you're probably letting them in because you booked them. A cold caller is a stranger. The photo match is the difference between "this person is who they claim to be" and "I'm opening my door to someone I can't identify." The most vulnerable targets of doorstep crime — elderly people, people living alone — are exactly the people who benefit most from a three-second photo match before engaging.

### The overlap

Some credentials appear in both stacks. Public liability insurance matters whether you booked the roofer or they knocked uninvited. A waste carrier licence matters whenever someone offers to take something away. The checking ritual is the same regardless — the stack just has different cards in it.

The point isn't that every visitor carries six cards. The point is that the *checking* becomes habitual and the *carrying* becomes expected.

## The Card is Theirs

A critical distinction: each card in the stack is **personal to the individual**, not to the company van they arrived in. The Gas Safe card has James Cooper's name, James Cooper's photo, and James Cooper's qualification categories. The verification response shows James Cooper's face — and the homeowner matches it to the person at the door.

This matters because the most common credential fraud in trades is the "gaffer's van" problem: a company holds a Gas Safe registration, but the person they send to your house is an unqualified labourer. The company's registration is genuine; the individual isn't on it. Personal cards with photo verification eliminate this. The question isn't "is Cooper Gas a registered company?" — it's "is *this person*, right now, on *this doorstep*, qualified to do *this work*?"

## The Swipe-Scan-Swipe Rhythm

**On phone (tradesperson's device):**

The tradesperson opens an app or a saved set of credential screens. Each screen shows the verifiable text for one credential. They hold the phone out. The customer scans with their own phone's camera. The tradesperson swipes to the next credential. The customer scans again. Five credentials in under 30 seconds.

**On laminated cards (ring binder):**

Each credential is a laminated card with the verifiable text printed in a clean, OCR-friendly format. The tradesperson flips to the first card, holds it up. The customer scans. Next card. Scan. The ring binder lives in the tradesperson's toolbag. It's as standard as their pipe wrench.

**On the van:**

Some credentials — waste carrier licence, employer ID — can be displayed as stickers or placards on the van itself. The customer scans while the tradesperson unloads tools. This works for the credentials that don't need photo verification (the van doesn't have a face).

## The Professional Norm

**Tradespeople should be keen.** A registered, qualified, insured tradesperson has nothing to hide and everything to gain from instant verification. Their credentials differentiate them from the cowboy operator who undercuts on price because they carry none of the registration, insurance, or tax burden.

The professional norm has three rules:

1. **Always present without being asked.** The tradesperson holds out the cards before the customer asks. Presenting credentials is part of arriving at the job, like putting on safety boots.

2. **Never comment on the checking.** No "you don't need to scan that." No "you checked it last year." No "my other customers don't bother." The checking is not a comment on trust — it's a procedure. A pilot doesn't say "you don't need to check the landing gear, we checked it this morning." The moment a tradesperson discourages verification is itself a red flag.

3. **Never hold the customer's phone.** The tradesperson presents their credentials. The customer scans with their own device. The verification happens on the customer's phone, against the issuer's domain. The tradesperson doesn't touch the verification flow.

## The Customer Habit

Customers grow accustomed to the batch check. The first time, it feels unusual. The third time, it's routine. By the tenth time, the *absence* of cards is what feels wrong. "Where's your Gas Safe card?" becomes as natural as "where's your invoice?"

The checking habit is reinforced by the photo return. Every scan shows a face. The customer matches it to the person at the door. This is satisfying — it's a visible, tangible confirmation that the system works. The green status is abstract; the matching face is concrete.

Over time, the credential stack becomes a **market signal**. Tradespeople who present a full stack of verified credentials get the jobs. Those who don't — who mumble about paperwork being at the office, or who discourage checking — lose to competitors who verify openly. The market does the enforcement that regulators can't.

## What Belongs in the Stack

Not every piece of paper a tradesperson holds is a verification candidate. The stack should contain credentials where:

- **The issuer is authoritative** — Gas Safe Register, Environment Agency, HMRC. Not self-issued certificates of completion from a weekend course.
- **The status can change** — Registrations expire, get suspended, get categories removed. A static qualification that never changes doesn't need live verification.
- **The customer is at risk** — Carbon monoxide from an unqualified gas fitter. Fly-tipping liability from an unlicensed waste carrier. VAT fraud from an unregistered trader. The credential exists because the customer needs protection.
- **The photo matters** — Personal credentials where the identity of the individual (not just the company) is the question. The face on the phone matches the face at the door.
