# Live Verify — Public Website: Pages, Content & Flows

This document describes the public-facing website of **Live Verify** for a reader (or model) that cannot open the live site or render HTML. It explains what each page *is*, what it *says*, and what it *does*. Live Verify is an open standard (Apache-2.0, begun by Paul Hammant, prompted by the PPE Medpro / Intertek forgery case) for verifying any document against its issuer. The core protocol, repeated almost verbatim across nearly every page, is: **`text → normalize → SHA-256 → GET the issuer's domain`**. There is no central database, no blockchain, no accounts, and no fees — a verification is a plain HTTPS lookup against the issuer's own server, which is precisely what lets a claim be *revoked* the moment the issuer changes its mind.

## Shared design system and recurring widgets

All pages share one **blue design system**: an off-white background (`#f6f8fb`) with a soft radial gradient at the top, white rounded "cards" with light borders and subtle shadows, a single blue accent (`#2563eb` / darker `#1d4ed8`, with a pale `#eff4ff` "accent-soft" fill), the system sans-serif font, and a monospace face for code. Recurring components: breadcrumbs ("Home › …"), a pill-shaped uppercase **badge** naming the audience, a blue "lede" paragraph, rounded blue CTA buttons (solid primary, outlined secondary), and a left-blue-bar "note" callout. Audience pages are **print-friendly** ("forward it to whoever owns this decision"). Every page footers with: *"Live Verify is an open standard begun by Paul Hammant · paul@hammant.org · Source & spec on GitHub."*

A shared script, **`slogan.js`**, appears on several pages (opportunity, one-way-hash, privacy_declaration). It powers two things on any element marked `data-slogan-root`:
1. A **toggle** on the line *"Live Verify: the next logical step up from the iPhone's Live Text."* A button (`<Android: Google Lens>` / `<iPhone: Live Text>`) lets the reader flip the framing between **iPhone Live Text** and **Android Google Lens** — swapping both the platform ("the iPhone's" ↔ "Android's") and the feature name.
2. A **rotating-strapline rotator** that cycles six value-proposition lines every 4 seconds, with clickable dots to jump between them. The six lines are: *"No phone calls. No upload portals. Just select, right-click, verified."*; *"Right-click to verify: degrees, receipts, licenses, employment letters."*; *"Turn camera OCR into instant anti-fraud checks."*; *"Just enough digital to make paper verifiable."*; *"Reduce point-of-use document fraud—without QR codes or upload portals."*; *"A path to removing massive fraud and waste from document workflows."*

## index.html — the landing page

**For:** everyone; the front door that routes visitors by role.
**Headline / voice:** *"Don't just trust. Verify."* with the subhead *"Verify any document against its source."* The lede: *"An open standard: select or snap the text, and a SHA-256 lookup tells you whether the issuer stands behind it right now — no central database, no blockchain."*

Sections in order:
- **The £122m story card** (`#why`): *"The £122m document nobody could check."* During the pandemic, PPE Medpro supplied the NHS against a safety test report bearing **Intertek's** name (report code `SHAT06648491`) that Intertek never issued; the forgery rode through 72 shipments before anyone could establish it was fake, and courts later ordered £122m repaid. *"If Intertek had published a hash of every genuine report on `intertek.com`, any procurement clerk could have caught the fake in four seconds, on day one."* This is the founding story and recurs across the site.
- **Audience "doors"** — four large clickable tiles routing to the audience pages: *"I issue documents"* → for-issuers; *"I build software that issues documents"* → for-saas; *"I build platforms"* → for-platforms; *"I sell verification today"* → for-verifiers (labelled "For incumbents & investors").
- **Secondary CTA row:** Quickstart, Clip Mode Examples, Use Cases, Opportunity, Privacy, Source Code.
- **"Forgery is now free"** (`#forgeryIsFree`) — the GenAI-urgency argument: *"anyone can ask a chatbot for a pixel-perfect insurance certificate, bank statement, employment letter, or test report … in under a minute, at zero cost. Every control that relies on a document looking right is already obsolete."* Detection is *"an arms race that defenders lose"*; attestation isn't, because a document either hashes to a value the issuer stands behind or it doesn't.
- **"How it works"** — a four-step pipeline diagram (Get text → Normalize → SHA-256 → Verify), distinguishing **Clip mode** (select the text) from **Camera mode** (snap and OCR). Documents carry a `verify:issuer.example.com/claims` line.
- **"A global anti-fraud system"** — frames the whole thing as a decentralized, DNS-rooted standard ("a standard, not a company"), with under ~195 sovereign national roots plus a few intergovernmental bodies (UN/WHO/WIPO), explicit "human in the loop" judgement, and an emphatic **"This is not a blockchain"** note.
- **Roadmap** (dark card) — two SVG tracks. *Client side:* Proof of concept (DONE) → Solid apps, iOS/Android/Chrome (DONE) → Platform adoption by Google/Apple/Microsoft/Samsung/Adobe/Mozilla/Opera (NEXT). *Issuer side:* PoC (DONE) → Scaled tech reference (DONE) → Pilot issuer deployments (NEXT) → 1,000 SaaS vendors (GOAL). A note points to the founding-adopter program.
- **Use-case category grid** — pulls live counts from `use-cases/index.json` (≈589 use cases across ~40 categories), each category tile linking into the use-case browser with a hover tooltip describing the fraud/safety/compliance concern.
- **"What 'Verified' means (and doesn't)"** — verified means only that the `verify:` domain currently attests the hash; it is *not* ground truth; the human judges the domain; watch for lookalike/typosquatted domains; verifications are revocable.
- **"Watch it work"** (dark "proof" section) — first **real iPhone/Android footage** (YouTube links) of a genuine peer reference and genuine-vs-fake police warrant cards verified on real hardware; then **simulated authority chains** captured by Playwright (fictitious orgs), shown as before/verified/authority-chain screenshot strips for Clip mode (bank statement → FDIC/Treasury, OFSI sanctions licence → gov.uk, pub & coffee-shop receipts → HMRC VAT, Scottish solicitor certs → Law Society/gov.scot, sanctions screening → FCA, a revoked employment reference) and Camera mode (genuine police ID verified, fake "Lex Luthor NYPD" rejected, Android OCR walkthroughs). Screenshots are clickable to open full-size.
- **Privacy model** — normalization + SHA-256 happen on-device; the only network step is the hash lookup; links to one-way-hash.html and the Privacy Declaration.

## The four audience pages (compared)

All four share the badge/lede/cards/CTA template and the same underlying protocol pitch, but each reframes it for a different reader. They cross-link to each other and converge on the **quickstart** and **founding-adopters** pages.

**`for-issuers.html` — "Your documents are being forged in your name."**
For organizations that *issue* documents (universities, regulators, insurers, certifiers, councils, hospitals). Argument: forgeries carry *your* name and today nobody can check them without phoning you. Costs you already pay: uncontrolled brand damage (the £122m story again), an unwanted "verification call centre," and no way to revoke. Adoption is *"smaller than you think"* — add one footer line (`verify:degrees.youruniversity.ac.uk/c`) and publish a SHA-256 per document on your own domain; *"Static file hosting is enough."* Effort: *"a sprint, not a migration."* Includes a callout for **small outfits** (a trades firm, parish council, one-person training provider) pointing straight to the quickstart, a *"your back catalog counts too"* retrospective-backfill section, a *"what you are not signing up for"* list (no PII leaves your systems, no vendor/fees/lock-in, no change to how documents look, no central registry), and a founding-issuer offer.

**`for-saas.html` — "Every document your product generates can carry proof it's real. Your competitors' can't."**
For software that issues documents on customers' behalf (invoicing, HR/payroll, insurance, credentialing, proptech, logistics, legal tech, health admin — shown as a per-vertical grid). The pitch is **competitive differentiation**: shipping verifiability is *"one footer line at render time plus a hash endpoint … a sales slide your competitors don't have."* A three-step honestly-scoped implementation (append `verify:` footer + hash at render → publish a static `/c/{hash}` returning `{"status":"verified"}` → wire revocation), a business case (differentiation that demos in 30 seconds, retention/lock-in, a ~100%-margin "verified documents" premium tier, co-branding), a **"second product: retrospective backfill"** (turn legacy records into endpoints), and a candid *"if it's an open standard, what do we own?"* answer (the rails are free; the value is integration, revocation workflow, compliance, uptime — the **Let's Encrypt** precedent). Founding-integration offer included.

**`for-platforms.html` — "Live Text told users what a document says. The next step is whether it's true."**
For camera, browser, OS and PDF vendors. Argument: the platform already does the hard part (on-device OCR, text selection), so issuer-attested verification is *"the increment"* and a natural "Verified / Revoked / Not found" overlay — *"the platform owns the gesture."* The timing is forced by free GenAI forgery; it's *"a safety feature, not a service"* (no accounts, no server to run). Stresses *"no gatekeeper to become, no gatekeeper to fear"* (DNS-rooted), privacy by construction, not-a-blockchain, and a deliberately small standard. Lists what already exists to take: Apache-2.0 reference implementations (iOS Vision+JavaScriptCore, Android ML Kit+Rhino, Manifest V3 extension), cross-platform conformance fixtures, real-hardware demos, and the mapped 589-use-case demand landscape. The explicit intent: *"The intent is for platforms to take this over."*

**`for-verifiers.html` — "If your business is verification, this page is the one written for you."**
The **incumbents & investors** page — addressed to anyone who *sells* verification today (degree checks, employment confirmations, document authentication, parts of KYC/background checks). It says the *"quiet part first"*: the rote *"is this real?"* lookup *"gets commoditized, the way Domain Validation certificates did after Let's Encrypt."* But the durable money is the work *around* the free rails — issuer registry & operations, retrospective backfill, integration into systems of record ("This is the moat"), compliance & liability wrapping, authority-chain anchoring, dispute/assurance — i.e. *"the DigiCert move."* A table contrasts losing defensive plays (FUD, name/standard capture, wait-and-see) against the one that works (*"Be the IdenTrust"* — lend early legitimacy, anchor a chain). A note for investors reframes the question to *"which side of it does our portfolio end up on?"* and points out the Let's Encrypt incumbents were largely PE-owned. Heavy cross-linking to the Let's Encrypt precedent write-up and to opportunity.html.

## founding-adopters.html — the founding adopter program

**For:** the first organizations willing to move early. Headline: *"The first ten get the author's ear. And this page, permanently."* **The deal:** the first ten pilot **issuers** and first ten **SaaS integrations** get free advisory support from the standard's author by email (design review of canonical text templates, normalization fixtures, endpoint setup, revocation workflow) *"up to a sensible advice limit"* — explicitly bounded as an evenings-and-weekends open-source project, responses in days not hours, sustained implementation referred to commercial partners — in exchange for being nameable. **Exit anytime** (delete files; no contract). The page shows numbered **slot lists**: one issuer slot is already **taken** ("Peer reference, live on a personal domain" — the first real deployment, with iPhone/Android video links); the rest are "Open." ## quickstart.html — the 15-minute walkthrough

**For:** anyone wanting to ship a verifiable claim hands-on. Headline: *"Publish your first verifiable claim before your coffee cools."* Five numbered step-cards:
1. **Write the claim** (~2 min) — a few short true lines plus a `verify:` line; the `verify:` line is the pointer and is excluded from the hash.
2. **Hash it** (~1 min) — paste into the **hash calculator**, which normalizes (whitespace, quotes, dashes) and shows the SHA-256; copy the hash.
3. **Publish the hash endpoint** (~8 min) — create a public GitHub repo, enable GitHub Pages, add one file `c/{hash}/index.html` containing `{"status":"verified"}`. *"That's the entire issuer-side infrastructure"* (GitHub Pages serves it with the CORS header clients need, free).
4. **Verify it** (~3 min) — open the URL (a 200 with the JSON is what clients check), then verify "the way a stranger would" via the browser extension (select → right-click → "Verify this claim" → green banner); edit one character and watch it fail; flip the file to `{"status":"revoked"}` to exercise revocation.
5. **What production adds** — own domain (authority lives in DNS), bulk hashing, richer statuses, authority chains, `verification-meta.json` normalization.

Cross-links back to for-issuers / for-saas and to the hash calculator.

## one-way-hash.html — the SHA-256 explainer

**For:** the skeptical/technical reader asking "is it safe to publish hashes?" Headline: *"One-way hashes stay safe in public."* Carries the slogan widget. Explains SHA-256 as a deterministic, collision-resistant, preimage-resistant one-way fingerprint; gives verifiable examples (`hello`, `hello world`, the King James Bible plain text); shows the astronomically large 2^256 keyspace and why brute-forcing is infeasible; distinguishes **collisions** from **reversing/preimage** (Live Verify relies on preimage resistance); recounts the 2017 **SHAttered** SHA-1 collision as the reason for moving to SHA-256; covers **issuer obligations** (treat plaintext as PCI-class sensitive, segment systems, possibly air-gap and publish a one-way feed), and **low-entropy inputs** — short/guessable claims are weak, so issuers add a **random salt line** (illustrated with an "Elvis Aaron Presley" sample driving-license credential) that becomes part of the normalized hashed text. CTAs to the Privacy Declaration and use cases.

## hash-calculator.html — interactive tool

**For:** anyone following the quickstart, or curious about normalization. It is a live, on-device tool (loads `normalize.js`, no network calls). Three stacked panels: a **textarea** for claim text; **"What you typed"** echoing the input with visible blue ↵ newline markers (so accidental/deliberate line breaks are obvious); an **"After normalization — this is what gets hashed"** panel showing the text post-`normalizeText()` (whitespace stripped per line, multiple spaces collapsed, blank lines removed, Unicode quotes/dashes normalized); and a **live SHA-256** output with a **Copy** button (turns green "Copied!"). It automatically strips a trailing `verify:`/`vfy:` line if pasted. Everything recomputes on each keystroke. This is the tool the quickstart's step 2 sends you to.

## privacy_declaration.html — the privacy stance

**For:** end users and anyone evaluating the privacy posture. Headline: *"Privacy Declaration."* Carries the slogan widget. **TL;DR:** for the prototype everything runs in the browser — OCR, normalization, hashing stay on-device; the only network calls are minimal HTTP GETs to check hashes (the same posture is expected when embedded in native iOS/Android camera apps). Sections: **Data Processing: 100% Local** (camera image not saved, on-device OCR via Apple Vision / Google ML Kit, local hashing); **Verifier roles, logging & retention** (distinguishes *document holder* from *verifier/operator*, with nightclub-door and law-enforcement examples — retention is verifier policy/law, not the protocol); a table of the only two request types (**metadata** GET for `verification-meta.json`, **hash verification** GET to `/c/{hash}` sending the hash only); an explicit **"What is NOT sent"** list (images, OCR text, video frames, PII, geolocation, device fingerprints); third-party resources (only the Public Suffix List from a CDN); **zero data retention**; browser permissions; open-source transparency; and a candid **"Verification Server Privacy"** note that the issuer's server may log request metadata, so hash lookups could leak that an ID was likely presented somewhere — *"not a weakness in hashing; it's ordinary web request metadata."*

## Cross-page flows (how a visitor moves)

- **Homepage doors → audience pages → action.** The four index doors route by role into for-issuers / for-saas / for-platforms / for-verifiers. Each audience page ends in CTAs that funnel to **quickstart.html** (issuers, SaaS), the **GitHub spec** (platforms), or the **Let's Encrypt precedent / opportunity.html** (verifiers), plus a "Talk about a pilot/integration" mailto.
- **Audience pages ↔ founding-adopters.** All audience pages and the homepage roadmap point to **founding-adopters.html**, which in turn links back to for-saas (vertical pitch) and to the quickstart ("Try it first").
- **Quickstart → hash-calculator.** Step 2 of the quickstart, and its primary CTA, open **hash-calculator.html**; the calculator is the concrete instance of the "normalize → SHA-256" step that every page describes abstractly.
- **Use-case footers → quickstart.** The ~589 use-case documents (browsable from the homepage grid and `use-cases/`) funnel readers toward the quickstart / founding-adopter path to actually deploy.
- **opportunity.html as a hub.** The wide-angle opportunity page contains its own "Where do you fit?" router to all four audience pages and the founding-adopter program, and shares the Let's Encrypt precedent and "Why Now?" arguments with for-saas, for-platforms, and for-verifiers.
- **Trust/credibility supporting pages.** index, privacy_declaration, and the audience pages all link to **one-way-hash.html** (why publishing hashes is safe) and **privacy_declaration.html** (on-device, hash-only) as the technical backstop for the "no PII, no central database" claims.

**Slogan / strapline.** The repeated positioning line is *"Live Verify: the next logical step up from the iPhone's Live Text"* (toggleable to *"Android's Google Lens"*), and the headline slogan across the site is *"Don't just trust. Verify."*
