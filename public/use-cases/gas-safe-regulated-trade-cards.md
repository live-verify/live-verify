## What is a Gas Safe ID Card?

Every gas engineer in the UK must be on the **Gas Safe Register** — it's a criminal offence to carry out gas work without registration. When a Gas Safe registered engineer arrives at your door, they carry a plastic ID card showing their name, photo, registration number, and the specific appliance categories they're qualified to work on (boilers, cookers, fires, LPG, etc.). The homeowner is supposed to check this card before letting them work.

Gas Safe replaced **CORGI** (Council for Registered Gas Installers) in 2009. CORGI had operated since 1970, and the name stuck in public consciousness — many homeowners still say "Are you CORGI registered?" a decade and a half after the change. The replacement happened because CORGI's enforcement was considered too weak; Gas Safe operates under a legal monopoly granted by the Health and Safety Executive (HSE).

The problem: Gas Safe ID cards are plastic cards with no anti-forgery features beyond a hologram. Fake cards are easily produced. The card shows the engineer's qualifications *at the time of printing* — if they've had categories removed, failed an inspection, or been struck off, the card doesn't update. The Gas Safe website offers a lookup (`gassaferegister.co.uk`) but nobody uses it at the doorstep. An unregistered gas fitter can kill a family with a faulty boiler installation — carbon monoxide doesn't smell, doesn't taste, and doesn't warn.

A **Verified Gas Safe Card** is the engineer's ID made scannable — the homeowner scans the card, and `gassaferegister.co.uk` returns the engineer's photo, current registration status, and the specific appliance categories they're currently qualified for. Not what was on the card when it was printed — what's true *right now*.

## The Photo Return

When the homeowner scans the card, the verification response includes the engineer's **photo from the register** — not the photo printed on the card. The homeowner looks at the person on their doorstep, looks at the face on their phone, and matches them. This is the identity binding.

The photo model is **always-on**: the engineer is on duty, at a customer's property, in a professional capacity. There is no privacy concern — the engineer *wants* to be verified. They're proud of their registration. The photo return is a feature, not an imposition. The card on the doorstep is *theirs* — their name, their face, their qualifications. It's not a company van badge that could belong to anyone in the fleet; it's a personal credential that says "I, James Cooper, am qualified to work on your boiler."

A good tradesperson will present their cards without being asked and will **never** discourage checking. "You don't need to scan that" or "you checked last time" are red flags. The professional norm is the opposite: the engineer holds out the card, the homeowner scans it, the photo comes back, they both move on. Three seconds. Every visit.

## Data Verified

Engineer's full name, registration number, registration body (Gas Safe, NICEIC, OFTEC, HETAS), employer/company name, expiry date, specific qualification categories (appliance types for gas, scope of work for electrical), registered address of employer.

**Document Types:**
- **Gas Safe ID Card:** The standard plastic card carried by every registered gas engineer. Shows registration number, employer, expiry, and appliance categories (boilers, cookers, fires, LPG, etc.).
- **NICEIC Approved Contractor Card:** Electrical contractors registered with the National Inspection Council for Electrical Installation Contracting. Categories include domestic installer, approved contractor, and specialist scopes (EV charging, solar PV).
- **OFTEC Registration Card:** Oil Firing Technical Association — for oil-fired heating engineers. Equivalent of Gas Safe for oil installations.
- **HETAS Registration Card:** Heating Equipment Testing and Approval Scheme — for solid fuel (wood burners, biomass boilers, chimneys).
- **MCS Certification Card:** Microgeneration Certification Scheme — for installers of renewable energy systems (solar PV, heat pumps, wind). Required for customers to claim government incentives.

## Data Visible After Verification

Shows the issuer domain (`gassaferegister.co.uk`, `niceic.com`, `oftec.org`, `hetas.co.uk`) and the engineer's current registration.

**Verification response includes:**
- **Photo** — Server-authoritative image of the registered engineer. The homeowner matches this to the person at the door.
- **Current status** — Registered, expired, suspended, or removed.
- **Qualification scope** — The specific work they're currently qualified to do. If the card says "boilers" but the register has removed that category since printing, the response shows the *current* scope.

**Status Indications:**
- **Registered** — Current registration, qualified for the categories shown.
- **Expired** — Registration has lapsed. Engineer should not be carrying out work.
- **Suspended** — Under investigation or failed an inspection. Work prohibited pending outcome.
- **Removed** — Struck off the register. Criminal offence to carry out gas work (Gas Safe). Serious safety concern.
- **Category Restricted** — Registered but one or more categories removed. Check the response carefully — they may be qualified for cookers but no longer for boilers.
- **Not Found** — No registration exists for this number. Do not allow this person to work on gas/electrical installations.

## Second-Party Use

The **Homeowner** benefits from verification.

**Before work begins:** You scan the card at the door. The photo matches the person in front of you. The status shows "Registered." The categories include "boilers." You let them in and they start work. Three seconds of checking prevents the scenario where an unqualified fitter installs a boiler that leaks carbon monoxide.

**Category check:** Your Gas Safe engineer is here to install a new gas fire. You scan their card. The response shows they're qualified for "boilers" and "cookers" but *not* "fires." They're registered — just not for this specific job. Without verification, you'd never know the card was printed before they lost that category.

**After the engineer leaves:** The engineer has gone but you want to confirm they were really registered before you sign off the landlord gas safety certificate. Scan the card details you noted down. The verification confirms they were registered at the time of the work.

## Third-Party Use

**Health and Safety Executive (HSE)**
**Enforcement:** The HSE is the regulator overseeing Gas Safe Register. Verified cards create a trail — when a carbon monoxide incident occurs, the HSE can confirm whether the engineer was registered and qualified for the work at the time it was done, not just whether they hold a current card.

**Landlords**
**Gas Safety Compliance:** Landlords must obtain an annual gas safety certificate (CP12) from a Gas Safe registered engineer for every rental property. A verified engineer card confirms the person who signed the CP12 was actually registered — closing the gap where landlords use cheap, unregistered fitters and forge the certificate.

**Insurance Companies**
**Claims Investigation:** After a gas explosion, fire, or carbon monoxide incident, the insurer checks: was the installation done by a registered engineer? A verified card trail proves the homeowner took reasonable steps. No verification trail — the insurer may dispute the claim.

**Local Authority Building Control**
**Notification Compliance:** Gas and electrical work must be notified to Building Control (or self-certified by a registered installer). Verified installer cards confirm the person who self-certified was actually qualified to do so.

**Letting Agents**
**Contractor Vetting:** Letting agents arranging maintenance on behalf of landlords can verify every contractor's registration before granting access to a tenant's home. The verification creates an auditable compliance record.

**Boiler/Appliance Manufacturers**
**Warranty Validation:** Worcester Bosch, Vaillant, and other manufacturers require installation by a Gas Safe registered engineer for warranty claims. A verified installer card at the time of installation proves the warranty condition was met — years later when the boiler fails.

## Verification Architecture

**The Unregistered Installer Problem**

Gas Safe conducts around 2,500 investigations per year into illegal gas work. They estimate that hundreds of thousands of gas jobs are done by unregistered fitters annually. The consequences are not abstract:

- **Carbon monoxide poisoning** kills around 30-40 people per year in the UK and hospitalises hundreds more. Many cases trace back to poorly installed or serviced gas appliances by unqualified fitters.
- **Gas explosions** destroy properties and kill occupants. The 2019 explosion in Andover, Hampshire — caused by an unregistered fitter — levelled a house and injured multiple people.
- **Fake Gas Safe cards** are produced for as little as £20 online. The hologram on the genuine card is the only anti-forgery feature, and it's easily replicated to a level that fools homeowners.
- **Expired or restricted registrations** — an engineer loses their boiler qualification after a failed inspection but continues working using their old card. The card looks valid; the register says otherwise.

**The CORGI Legacy Problem**

CORGI was decommissioned in 2009 but the brand is still widely recognised. Some rogue operators trade on the name: "CORGI registered" sounds legitimate to homeowners who haven't heard of Gas Safe. CORGI now operates as a commercial brand (corgi.com) offering boiler cover and home emergency services — completely separate from gas safety registration. The brand confusion is itself a fraud vector.

**Issuer Types** (First Party)

**Gas Safe Register** (`gassaferegister.co.uk`) — Legal monopoly holder for gas registration in Great Britain and Isle of Man. Operated by Capita (under contract from HSE). Approximately 130,000 registered engineers.

**NICEIC** (`niceic.com`) — The leading electrical contractor registration body. Operates as a Competent Person Scheme, meaning registered contractors can self-certify notifiable electrical work without involving Building Control.

**OFTEC** (`oftec.org`) — Oil Firing Technical Association. Registration body for oil heating engineers. Approximately 9,000 registered technicians.

**HETAS** (`hetas.co.uk`) — Heating Equipment Testing and Approval Scheme. Registration body for solid fuel installers (wood burners, biomass, chimneys).

**MCS** (`mcscertified.com`) — Microgeneration Certification Scheme. Certification body for renewable energy installers. Required for customers to access the Boiler Upgrade Scheme and Smart Export Guarantee.

## Rationale

Gas Safe registration is one of the few trade credentials where failure to verify can directly result in death. The system already works — there is a register, there are ID cards, there is a website lookup. The gap is purely at the point of encounter: the doorstep, where the homeowner meets the engineer and has three seconds to decide whether to let them in. A scannable card that returns a photo and a live status is the difference between "I'll take your word for it" and "I can see you're registered, come in." The verification cost is trivial; the cost of not verifying is measured in lives.


## Jurisdictional Witnessing

A jurisdiction may require the issuer to retain a **witnessing firm** for regulatory compliance. The witnessing firm:

- Receives all hashes from the issuer, and any subsequent changes to the payload as they happen—which may manifest as a new hash, a status change, or even a 404 (record deleted)
- Receives structured content/metadata (key identifiers and dates)
- Does **NOT** receive plaintext or sensitive personal information
- Provides an immutable, timestamped audit trail—available to the jurisdiction on demand, to document holders/third parties during disputes, or as expert witness testimony in legal proceedings

This provides:
- **Non-repudiation:** Issuer cannot deny issuing the document
- **Timestamp proof:** Document existed at a specific time
- **Regulatory audit:** Jurisdictions can inspect the witness ledger for fraud detection
- **Resilience:** Verification works even if issuer's systems go down

**Public Blockchain (Non-Party)**

Witnessing firms may periodically commit rollups to an inexpensive public blockchain, providing an ultimate immutability guarantee. The blockchain is a "non-party"—infrastructure, not a participant in the transaction. This creates multiple verification paths:

1. **Issuer domain** — Direct check against the issuer
2. **Witnessing firm** — Independent confirmation with timestamp
3. **Public blockchain** — Decentralized trust anchor via rollup inclusion
