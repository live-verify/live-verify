## The Problem

A reader sees a photograph in a news story, a product image on a marketplace, or a clip in a feed,
and has no reliable way to ask the most basic question now hanging over every piece of media: **was
this made by a camera and a person, or generated by a model?** Synthetic images, cloned voices, and
machine-written articles are now indistinguishable to the eye, and the few signals that did exist —
metadata, watermarks, file headers — are stripped the moment content is screenshotted, re-encoded,
or re-hosted. A disclosure that lives *inside* the file does not survive the trip to the reader.

Publishers, newsrooms, stock-photo houses, and model vendors increasingly *want* to disclose origin —
both to comply with emerging rules (the EU AI Act's transparency obligations, platform labelling
mandates) and to protect their own brand against "is this real?" doubt. But a disclosure is only
worth anything if a downstream reader can confirm it came from the named source and was not forged or
flipped. A scammer can paste "Camera-Original, verified by Reuters" under a fabricated image just as
easily as Reuters can paste it under a real one.

This use case binds a provenance *statement* — "this asset is AI-generated by model X" or "this asset
is camera-original and unedited" — to the **issuer's own domain** via a hash, so a reader holding the
asset (and the short disclosure beside it) can confirm the named publisher actually made that claim.
It is the sibling of [Ad Placement Provenance](ad-placement-provenance.md): both answer "who stands
behind what you are looking at?" rather than inspecting the pixels themselves.

It is **complementary** to C2PA / Content Credentials and to AI-detection tooling, not a replacement.
C2PA embeds a signed manifest in the file; detection tools guess from the pixels. Live Verify adds a
third leg that survives screenshotting and re-hosting: a short human-readable disclosure whose hash
resolves against the issuer's domain even after every embedded credential has been stripped away.

## What gets attested

The issuer publishes a short **provenance disclosure** alongside the asset (in the caption, the alt
text, an adjacent panel, or a "provenance" link), and registers its hash on their domain. The
disclosure states, in plain terms:

- the **asset** it describes (a stable reference / perceptual identifier for the image, article, or clip);
- the **origin classification** — `AI-GENERATED` (with the model named) **or** `CAMERA-ORIGINAL / HUMAN-AUTHORED, UNEDITED` — and nothing in between is implied;
- the **issuer** making the claim (the newsroom, stock house, or model vendor);
- the **as-at date** of the disclosure;
- a **salt** so the short, low-entropy statement cannot be guess-and-hashed.

What this attests is **who declared what about the content's origin** — it is not a forensic finding.
A dishonest issuer can mis-declare an AI image as camera-original exactly as a dishonest issuer can
mis-declare anything else; the trust assumption is the issuer's domain reputation, the same boundary
that applies to every Live Verify credential. What the mechanism removes is *anonymous* and *forged*
disclosure: a claim is now pinned to a named, accountable domain, and a third party cannot fake "by
Reuters" without Reuters' endpoint confirming it.

A picture editor preparing to run the image clips the disclosure and verifies it. The endpoint at
`wirephoto.example` confirms the issuer published exactly this statement — `AI-GENERATED`, by the
named model, as at this date — so the editor knows to label it as illustrative rather than present it
as documentary. Had the same disclosure read `CAMERA-ORIGINAL / HUMAN-AUTHORED, UNEDITED`, the
verification would confirm the issuer stood behind *that* claim instead. Either way the reader learns
what the named source declared; the endpoint confirms authenticity and status, and never echoes the
asset back.

## Data Verified

The asset reference (and perceptual identifier), the origin classification (`AI-GENERATED` with model
name, or `CAMERA-ORIGINAL / HUMAN-AUTHORED, UNEDITED`), the issuing publisher/vendor, the as-at date,
the disclosure text, and the salt. The hash binds *which named issuer declared which origin for this
asset* — it does **not** bind, prove, or detect the actual generative process behind the pixels.

**What is deliberately NOT included:**

- any forensic "this is/ isn't AI" verdict derived from the content itself (this is attestation, not detection)
- the asset's pixels, bytes, or full text (the verifier already holds the asset; the endpoint never echoes it)
- the prompt, training data, or internal generation parameters
- any reader- or viewer-identifying data

## Data Visible After Verification

The issuer domain (`wirephoto.example`, a newsroom, a model vendor) and the standing of the
disclosure for the named asset.

**Status Indications:**

- **Disclosed — AI-Generated** — the issuer confirms this asset is machine-generated, by the named model.
- **Disclosed — Camera-Original / Human-Authored** — the issuer confirms this asset is unedited human/camera origin.
- **Superseded** — a corrected disclosure has replaced this one (e.g. an asset re-classified after an editorial review); the reader is pointed to the current statement.
- **Withdrawn / Revoked** — the issuer has retracted the disclosure (e.g. the asset was found to be misattributed); the prior claim no longer stands.
- **No Disclosure (404)** — no disclosure for this asset/hash exists on the issuer's domain. The reader is told this plainly — *no provenance is asserted* — and is **never** shown a fabricated "looks genuine" or "looks AI" result in its place (fail-loudly).

## Second-Party Use

The **issuing publisher, newsroom, stock house, or model vendor** benefits directly.

- **Defends the brand against blanket doubt.** As "is this real?" becomes the default reaction to every image, a source that can let readers *confirm* its disclosure converts suspicion into a checkable claim — and distinguishes itself from outlets that simply assert.
- **Meets transparency obligations durably.** Where law or platform policy requires AI content to be labelled, a domain-bound disclosure satisfies the requirement in a form that survives re-hosting, where an embedded-only credential would not.
- **Protects camera-original work too.** A photojournalist's unedited frame can carry a positive `CAMERA-ORIGINAL` disclosure, giving real reportage a verifiable provenance claim of its own rather than leaving it indistinguishable from synthetic imitations.

## Third-Party Use

**Readers, editors, and fact-checkers**

The core action: clip the disclosure beside an asset, confirm the named source actually classified it
as AI-generated or camera-original. Attribution on demand — never a detection verdict.

**Platforms and aggregators**

A platform re-surfacing third-party content can verify the publisher's disclosure at ingestion and
attach a confirmed, source-bound label rather than guessing with a detector. It pairs naturally with
C2PA: if an embedded credential is present, use it; if it was stripped in transit, fall back to the
domain-bound disclosure.

**Newsrooms and wire services**

A picture desk receiving syndicated imagery can confirm the originating agency's origin classification
before publishing, rather than trusting a stripped caption.

**Regulators and consumer-protection bodies**

When investigating a deceptive synthetic-media campaign, a confirmed disclosure (or its plain absence)
names what the publisher declared, without the regulator having to reverse-engineer the file.

## Verification Architecture

The disclosure is a short, low-entropy human-readable statement bound to the asset and registered on
the issuer's domain. Verification is a deliberate **clip-style action**: the verifier already holds
the asset and the disclosure text, clips the disclosure, and the pipeline normalises it, takes the
SHA-256, and issues a GET against the issuer's `/provenance/` endpoint, which returns status only and
never echoes content.

- The classification is **whatever the issuer declared** — `AI-GENERATED` or `CAMERA-ORIGINAL`. The mechanism confirms the *declaration's* authenticity and current standing, not the underlying generative truth. This boundary is stated to users plainly: provenance disclosure ≠ AI detection.
- Because the asset reference and origin line are short and predictable, the salt is **load-bearing** (see below) — without it the small statement space could be enumerated.
- It composes with existing controls: C2PA supplies an embedded signed manifest; AI-detection tools supply a pixel-level guess; Live Verify supplies a domain-bound disclosure that outlives stripping and re-hosting. None replaces the others.

This is explicitly a *provenance and accountability* mechanism. It does not detect deepfakes; it
ensures that when a source discloses an origin, that disclosure is pinned to a named, accountable
domain rather than floating as forgeable caption text.

## Privacy Salt

The salt is required. The disclosure is short and its origin line is one of a small set of values, so
without a per-disclosure salt an attacker could pre-compute hashes for likely asset/classification
pairs and either confirm a private editorial decision or forge a match. Each disclosure carries a
unique salt so that:

- the statement cannot be guess-and-hashed from a known asset reference and a guessed classification;
- two disclosures for related assets produce unrelated hashes (no enumeration of an issuer's catalogue by hash);
- the reveal exposes the *issuer's claim about the asset*, never anything about the reader who checked it.

## Authority Chain

**Pattern:** Commercial (or Regulated where a labelling mandate applies).

The chain terminates at the **issuing publisher, newsroom, stock house, or model vendor's own
domain** — the party that made and stands behind the origin claim. It expresses *disclosure
accountability* rather than regulatory authorisation, though a regulator (e.g. under an AI
transparency mandate) may sit above the issuer where labelling is compelled by law.

```
✓ wirephoto.example — Issuer of the provenance disclosure (accountable anchor)
  ✓ aperture-diffusion.example — Model vendor named in the AI-generated classification
```

Self-authorised commercial issuer: trust rests on the issuer's domain reputation, exactly as it does
for any other Live Verify issuer — which is also why a lying issuer can mis-declare, and why this is
attestation of origin, not forensic detection of it.
