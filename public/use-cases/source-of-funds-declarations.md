## What is a Source of Funds Declaration?

When opening a high-value account or making a significant investment, financial institutions must understand where your money comes from. The **source of funds declaration** documents:

- **Source of Funds:** Where did this specific transaction money come from? (e.g., sale of property, salary, inheritance)
- **Source of Wealth:** How did you accumulate your overall wealth? (e.g., business ownership, investments, inheritance)

This is core AML (anti-money laundering) compliance. If you can't explain where money came from, the institution can't accept it.

## Data Verified

**Client name**, **transaction/account reference**, **declaration date**, **source description**, **amount**, **supporting document references**, **signatory confirmation**.

## Data Visible After Verification

**Status Indications:**
- **Accepted** — Declaration reviewed and accepted by firm
- **Under Review** — Additional documentation requested
- **Superseded** — Updated declaration provided (e.g., source changed)
- **Rejected** — Declaration not accepted (serious — may trigger SAR)

## Why This Matters

**Money laundering basics:** Criminals need to "explain" dirty money. Fake source of funds declarations — claiming money came from property sales, business profits, or inheritance when it didn't — are a primary laundering technique.

**Red flags:**
- Funds that don't match stated occupation/income
- Vague explanations ("savings", "investments" without detail)
- Supporting documents that don't verify
- Multiple declarations with changing stories

## Jurisdiction Differences

| Jurisdiction | Regulator | Framework | Key Requirements |
|--------------|-----------|-----------|------------------|
| **UK** | FCA | MLR 2017, JMLSG Guidance | SOF required for high-value transactions; SOW for ongoing relationship |
| **US** | FinCEN | BSA, CDD Rule | Beneficial ownership + source of funds for high-risk customers |
| **EU** | National FIUs | AMLD 4/5/6 | SOF/SOW for enhanced due diligence cases |
| **Offshore** | Various | FATF standards | Often stricter than onshore (reputational pressure) |

UK wealth managers typically require SOW for all private banking clients; US has less prescriptive rules but similar practical requirements.

**Further Jurisdictional Peers**

- **Australia (AUSTRAC)** — Under the *Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorism Financing Act 2006* and AUSTRAC's AML/CTF Rules, reporting entities must collect source of funds information as part of customer due diligence, particularly for enhanced due diligence on high-risk customers and politically exposed persons. Key difference: Australia's SOF requirements are principles-based rather than prescriptive — AUSTRAC expects firms to collect SOF/SOW proportionate to the assessed risk, without mandating specific forms or thresholds.
- **Singapore (MAS)** — MAS requires source of funds and source of wealth declarations under *MAS Notice 626* (for banks) and equivalent notices for other financial institution types. SOF/SOW must be obtained for all customers assessed as higher risk, including PEPs and customers from high-risk jurisdictions. Key difference: Singapore's regime is notably detailed — Notice 626 specifies that firms must corroborate SOF/SOW through independent sources, not merely accept client self-declarations.
- **Japan (JAFIC)** — Under the *Act on Prevention of Transfer of Criminal Proceeds*, financial institutions must verify the source of funds for transactions exceeding JPY 2 million (~GBP 10,000) and for all high-risk customers. JAFIC guidelines require documented SOF/SOW for enhanced due diligence cases. Key difference: Japan's threshold-based approach triggers SOF requirements at specific transaction amounts, contrasting with the UK's risk-based model.
- **Canada (FINTRAC)** — Under the *Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) and Terrorist Financing Act (PCMLTFA)* and FINTRAC guidance, reporting entities must determine the source of funds and source of wealth for high-risk clients, PEPs, and large cash transactions. Key difference: Canada requires SOF for all large cash transactions (CAD 10,000+) regardless of risk level, and enhanced SOF/SOW for all PEP relationships; FINTRAC's 2024 guidance strengthened requirements for virtual currency transactions.
- **Switzerland (FINMA)** — Source of funds requirements are enshrined in the *Anti-Money Laundering Act (AMLA)* and the *Agreement on the Swiss Banks' Code of Conduct (CDB 20)*. Financial intermediaries must establish the economic background and purpose of transactions that appear unusual, and must document SOF/SOW for all business relationships. Key difference: Switzerland's CDB 20 imposes a "declaration of beneficial ownership" requirement at account opening and requires ongoing SOF monitoring — Swiss private banks are expected to maintain detailed SOW profiles for all wealth management clients.
- **Hong Kong (HKMA)** — The Hong Kong Monetary Authority requires SOF/SOW under the *Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorist Financing Ordinance (AMLO)* and HKMA's AML/CFT guidelines. Authorized institutions must obtain SOF/SOW for enhanced due diligence cases, PEPs, and high-risk business relationships. Key difference: Hong Kong's guidelines emphasise that SOF documentation should be proportionate to the size and complexity of the client's wealth — private banking clients require more detailed SOW profiles than retail customers.

**Bribery & Corruption**

Source of funds declarations are a primary tool for detecting proceeds of bribery and corruption — PEPs and their associates must explain wealth that may derive from corrupt activity.

- **UK:** Unexplained Wealth Orders (UWOs) under the Criminal Finances Act 2017 — the NCA can require a SOF explanation for assets over £50,000 held by PEPs or suspected criminals; reverses the burden of proof onto the asset holder
- **US:** DOJ Kleptocracy Asset Recovery Initiative — uses civil forfeiture to seize corruption proceeds parked in the US; SOF declarations at financial institutions are a primary source of evidence in these investigations
- **France:** HATVP (Haute Autorité pour la Transparence de la Vie Publique) — public officials must declare assets and sources of wealth; discrepancies between declared SOW and lifestyle trigger corruption investigations
- **Australia:** NACC (National Anti-Corruption Commission, est. 2023) — federal jurisdiction over proceeds of corruption; state-level unexplained wealth laws (e.g., NSW CARA 1990) allow confiscation without criminal conviction
- **Switzerland:** FINMA AML rules require enhanced SOF for all PEP relationships; Swiss authorities have frozen billions in kleptocratic assets (notably from former leaders of Nigeria, Egypt, Tunisia, and Ukraine)
- **Singapore:** CPIB — the 2023 money laundering crackdown (S$3 billion seized) involved intensive SOF scrutiny of foreign nationals; demonstrated that SOF controls are as much an anti-corruption tool as an AML one
- **International:** Stolen Asset Recovery Initiative (StAR) — World Bank/UNODC partnership for recovering assets stolen through corruption; relies on SOF/SOW documentation held by financial institutions in destination countries

**Tax Evasion & Transparency**

Source of funds declarations catch tax evasion (undeclared income, offshore funds) as well as money laundering. Tax enforcement uses distinct legal frameworks from AML:

- **FATCA/CRS:** Source of funds declarations must align with reported income; discrepancies trigger tax authority referrals
- **UK:** HMRC offshore income disclosure facilities; failure to declare offshore income is a criminal offence under Criminal Finances Act 2017
- **US:** IRS Criminal Investigation (IRS-CI) — largest financial crime investigation agency; SOF scrutiny for unreported foreign accounts (FBAR violations)
- **Switzerland:** End of banking secrecy for tax purposes — automatic exchange of information under CRS since 2018; SOF declarations now verified against CRS data
- **Australia:** ATO Serious Financial Crimes Taskforce — SOF scrutiny for offshore arrangements; Project Wickenby legacy
- **Singapore:** Exchange of information under CRS since 2018; MAS requires SOF for accounts with tax-risk indicators

**Trade-Based Money Laundering (TBML)**

Proceeds of trade-based laundering are often declared as "trade profits" or "export income" in source of funds declarations — the declared source is technically accurate (the money did come from trade) but the underlying trade was fraudulent. TBML is enforced by customs authorities rather than financial regulators.

- **Detection challenge:** TBML-derived funds are harder to flag than cash or wire-based laundering because they come with supporting trade documentation (invoices, bills of lading, customs declarations) that appear legitimate — the SOF declaration and its supporting documents both look genuine
- **Verification opportunity:** Live Verify could authenticate the trade documents cited in SOF declarations — if the invoice, bill of lading, or customs declaration is itself verifiable against the issuer's domain, TBML becomes much harder to conceal
- **FATF:** TBML is specifically addressed in FATF guidance (2006, updated 2020) as one of three main money laundering methods; source of funds controls are identified as a key detection point because TBML proceeds must eventually enter the financial system with an explanation

**Unexplained Wealth & Asset Recovery**

Unexplained wealth orders, civil forfeiture, and non-conviction-based confiscation are distinct from AML — they reverse the burden of proof, allowing authorities to seize assets without a criminal conviction. SOF declarations are the direct target: the subject must explain where their wealth came from.

- **UK:** Unexplained Wealth Orders (UWOs) under Criminal Finances Act 2017 — NCA/law enforcement can require persons connected with serious crime or PEPs to explain assets over £50,000; failure to provide satisfactory explanation leads to civil recovery; high-profile cases include Zamira Hajiyeva (£22M Harrods spending)
- **US:** Civil Asset Forfeiture under 18 USC §981, 21 USC §881; DOJ Kleptocracy Asset Recovery Initiative (est. 2010) — has recovered >$2B in assets; no UWO equivalent but civil forfeiture effectively reverses burden of proof
- **Australia:** Unexplained wealth laws in multiple states (WA Criminal Property Confiscation Act 2000 was first; federal Criminal Code s.179E); Australian Criminal Intelligence Commission (ACIC) coordinates; considered world-leading in non-conviction-based confiscation
- **EU:** Directive 2014/42/EU on freezing and confiscation; proposed 2022 directive on asset recovery and confiscation would introduce non-conviction-based confiscation EU-wide; Italy's anti-mafia confiscation regime (Rognoni–La Torre Act 1982) is the pioneer
- **Canada:** Civil forfeiture legislation in most provinces (BC Civil Forfeiture Act, Ontario Civil Remedies Act); federal Proceeds of Crime (Money Laundering) provisions under Criminal Code Part XII.2
- **France:** AGRASC (Agence de Gestion et de Recouvrement des Avoirs Saisis et Confisqués) — manages seized/confiscated assets; non-conviction-based confiscation under Code de Procédure Pénale
- **Switzerland:** Federal Act on the Sharing of Confiscated Assets (LVCA); MROS can freeze assets for 5 days; Swiss courts have ordered confiscation of kleptocratic assets (Abacha case — $700M returned to Nigeria)
- **Singapore:** Corruption, Drug Trafficking and Other Serious Crimes (Confiscation of Benefits) Act (CDSA); 2023 money laundering case saw $3B+ in assets seized — largest confiscation in Singapore's history
- **Ireland:** Criminal Assets Bureau (CAB) — pioneered non-conviction-based confiscation under Proceeds of Crime Act 1996; model adopted by several other jurisdictions
- **International:** StAR (Stolen Asset Recovery Initiative — World Bank/UNODC); UNCAC (UN Convention Against Corruption) Art. 51-59 on asset recovery; Camden Asset Recovery Inter-Agency Network (CARIN)

## Verification Architecture

**The Problem:**
- Clients provide forged supporting documents (fake solicitor letters, altered bank statements)
- Declarations are altered after acceptance to change the story
- Firms claim they obtained declarations that don't exist
- Clients deny making declarations that they did make

**The Fix:** Declaration hashed at signing, with references to supporting documents (which may have their own hashes). If story changes later, original declaration is verifiable. Supporting documents from third parties (solicitors, employers) can also be verified against their domains.

**The chain:** Client declares source → Supporting doc from third party (e.g., employer's salary confirmation) verified against employer's domain → Firm's acceptance of declaration verified against firm's domain. Multiple verification points make fabrication much harder.

## Jurisdictional Witnessing

A jurisdiction may require the issuer to retain a **witnessing firm** for regulatory compliance. The witnessing firm:

- Receives all hashes from the issuer, and any subsequent changes to the payload as they happen—which may manifest as a new hash, a status change, or even a 404 (record deleted)
- Receives structured content/metadata (key identifiers and dates)
- Does **NOT** receive plaintext or sensitive personal information
- Provides an immutable, timestamped audit trail—available to the jurisdiction on demand, to document holders/third parties during disputes, or as expert witness testimony in legal proceedings

This provides:
- **Non-repudiation:** Issuer cannot deny issuing the document
- **Timestamp proof:** Document existed at a specific time
- **Regulatory audit:** Jurisdictions can inspect the witness ledger for fraud detection
- **Resilience:** Verification works even if issuer's systems go down

**Jurisdictional Requirements (Offshore Jurisdictions)**

Documents issued from offshore financial centers (Cayman Islands, Jersey, Guernsey, Sark, BVI, Panama, etc.) face heightened regulatory scrutiny due to international anti-money laundering (AML) and FATCA compliance frameworks.

**US Foreign Policy Requirement:**
When documents from these jurisdictions are shared internationally or used in US transactions, US foreign policy strongly recommends independent witnessing firms that are:
- **NOT located in the same jurisdiction** as the issuer
- Preferably located in neutral OECD jurisdictions (UK, Switzerland, EU member states)
- Maintaining separate custody of audit trails to prevent collusion with the primary issuer

This creates an independent verification chain that satisfies the OECD's Common Reporting Standard (CRS) and US FATCA requirements for cross-border financial transparency.

**Public Blockchain (Non-Party)**

Witnessing firms may periodically commit rollups to an inexpensive public blockchain, providing an ultimate immutability guarantee. The blockchain is a "non-party"—infrastructure, not a participant in the transaction. This creates multiple verification paths:

1. **Issuer domain** — Direct check against the issuer
2. **Witnessing firm** — Independent confirmation with timestamp
3. **Public blockchain** — Decentralized trust anchor via rollup inclusion
