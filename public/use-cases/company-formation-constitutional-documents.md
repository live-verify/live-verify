## The Problem

A company's foundational documents — its Certificate of Incorporation, its Memorandum & Articles of Association (or Articles of Incorporation / Bylaws in the US), and its statutory registers of shareholders and directors — are the most frequently requested and most frequently forged corporate documents in the world.

**Bank account opening:** Every bank requires a Certificate of Incorporation and Articles of Association before opening a corporate account. The bank receives a PDF. It has no way to confirm the PDF matches what the registry actually holds. Fraudsters create convincing certificates for companies that don't exist, or alter genuine certificates to change the company name, number, or directors.

**Investment due diligence:** Before investing, a VC or PE firm needs to see the Articles to understand share classes, voting rights, pre-emption clauses, and drag-along provisions. They receive a copy from the company. If the company has amended its Articles since the last filing, the investor may be reading a stale or selectively edited version.

**Shareholder disputes:** "I own 30% of this company" is a claim that depends on the Register of Members. In private companies, this register is maintained by the company itself (or its company secretary). There is no instant way for a third party to verify the current shareholding structure. Disputes over share ownership in private companies are common, expensive, and slow to resolve.

**Director authority:** "I am a director of this company and I am authorized to sign this contract" — the counterparty checks Companies House or the Secretary of State's website, but the online record may lag by days or weeks. A recently removed director may still appear as current. A recently appointed director may not yet appear.

The underlying records exist at the registry, but they travel as unverifiable PDFs, photocopies, and printouts. The gap between what the registry holds and what the counterparty receives is where fraud lives.

## 2. Memorandum & Articles of Association

The constitution of a company. Defines its objects, share structure, director powers, voting rules, and transfer restrictions. In the US, the equivalent is Articles of Incorporation (filed with the state) plus Bylaws (internal governance rules).

Unlike the Certificate of Incorporation, the Articles change. Companies amend them — sometimes frequently. The version that matters is the one currently on file at the registry, not the one the company hands you.

**Why this matters:** An investor reading the Articles wants to know about pre-emption rights, drag-along clauses, and share class voting weights. If the company has quietly filed amended Articles removing pre-emption rights, the investor needs to see the version currently on file — not the version the company chose to share. Verification confirms "this is the text currently held by the registry."

**The Amendment Problem:** Articles are amended by special resolution. The company files the amended Articles with the registry. But anyone holding a copy of the old Articles may not know an amendment occurred. With Live Verify, the old Articles would return SUPERSEDED, prompting the reader to obtain the current version.

**Multi-Page Hashing:** Articles of Association are typically 10–30 pages. The Merkle root approach allows verification of the complete document: each page is hashed, and the page hashes are combined into a single root hash. The verifier can confirm the entire document matches what was filed, or identify which specific page has been altered.

**The Dispute Scenario:** David Kwan claims he owns 40% of the company. Helen Ashford says it's 25%. The verified Register of Members — the version held by the registry or the company's official register maintained by its company secretary — settles the question. **Snapshot Nature:** The Register of Members changes over time as shares are transferred, issued, or cancelled. Each extract is a snapshot. Verification confirms "this was the register at this date." When the register changes, the previous extract returns SUPERSEDED and a new hash is published for the current state.

**The Authority Question:** "I am a director and I am authorized to sign this lease." The counterparty clips the Register of Directors from the company's filing and verifies. If the person is listed as CURRENT, they have standing. If FORMER, they do not. This is faster than navigating to Companies House, searching, and cross-referencing — and it produces a verification result that can be recorded as part of the transaction audit trail.

**Registry Lag:** In many jurisdictions, director changes take days or weeks to appear on the public register. During this window, a removed director may still appear as current. Live Verify can only be as fresh as the registry's data. The verification response should include the "as at" date so the verifier knows the snapshot age.

## Data Verified

**Certificate of Incorporation:** Company name, company number, jurisdiction, date of incorporation, company type (limited by shares, limited by guarantee, corporation, LLC, etc.), registered office location.

**Memorandum & Articles:** Company name and number, date of adoption/amendment, full text of articles (via Merkle root for multi-page documents), share capital structure, director powers, transfer restrictions, voting rules.

**Register of Members:** Company name and number, extract date, each shareholder's name, share class and quantity, percentage holding, date of registration, transfer history.

**Register of Directors:** Company name and number, extract date, each director's name, role/title, appointment date, resignation date (if applicable), nationality, month/year of birth, current/former status.

**Document Types:**
- **Certificate of Incorporation** — One-time formation document.
- **Articles of Association / Memorandum & Articles** — Constitutional document, may be amended. UK/Commonwealth terminology.
- **Articles of Incorporation** — Filed constitutional document. US terminology (state-level).
- **Bylaws** — Internal governance rules. US terminology (not filed with the state, but may be verified by the company's registered agent or law firm).
- **Register of Members Extract** — Snapshot of current shareholding.
- **Register of Directors Extract** — Snapshot of current and former directors.
- **Share Allotment Return** — Filed when new shares are issued (UK: SH01 form).
- **Annual Return / Confirmation Statement** — Periodic filing confirming register accuracy.
- **Trust Deed / Deed of Settlement** — Constitutional document of a trust, naming settlor, trustees, beneficiaries, and governing law.
- **Structure Chart (UBO Diagram)** — Signed ownership diagram showing the chain from investing entity to Ultimate Beneficial Owner.
- **Professional Certification Letter** — Attestation by a notary, lawyer, or accountant that copies are true reproductions of originals.
- **Legal Opinion (Source of Wealth)** — Law firm opinion on the legality of a client's business activities and source of wealth, typically required for FATF Grey/Black List jurisdictions.

**Privacy Salt:** Required for Register of Members and Register of Directors when they include personal details (date of birth, residential address). The registry may salt personal details so that the hash can be verified without exposing PII to intermediaries. The verification response confirms the register is authentic without revealing individual shareholder addresses. Trust Deeds require particular care — beneficiary names and trust property details are sensitive. The hash covers the full text, but the verification response reveals only status.

## Data Visible After Verification

Shows the issuer domain (e.g. `companieshouse.gov.uk`, `corp.delaware.gov`) and the document status.

**Status Indications:**
- **Verified** — Document matches the registry's current record.
- **Superseded** — Document has been replaced by a newer version (amended Articles, updated register extract). The old document was genuine at its date but is no longer current.
- **Dissolved** — Company has been dissolved; documents are historical records only.
- **Struck Off** — Company removed from the register for non-compliance.
- **404** — No such document was filed with this registry.

## Second-Party Use

The **company** benefits from verification.

**Faster bank account opening:** Instead of a bank manually inspecting a PDF certificate of incorporation and phoning Companies House to confirm, the bank clips the verified certificate. Account opening that takes weeks can be reduced to hours.

**Investor confidence:** A company raising a funding round can provide verified Articles showing the exact share structure, pre-emption rights, and voting provisions. The investor doesn't need to wonder whether the copy they received matches what was filed.

**Contract execution:** When signing a lease, loan agreement, or major contract, the company can demonstrate that its directors are current and authorized. The counterparty has a verification record, not just a printout.

**Share transfer certainty:** When a shareholder sells their stake, the buyer can verify the seller's holding against the Register of Members before exchanging funds.

## Third-Party Use

**Banks and Financial Institutions**

**KYC/AML compliance:** Verified incorporation documents and director registers are foundational to Know Your Customer processes. A verified certificate confirms the entity exists, and a verified register confirms who controls it. This is stronger evidence than an unverifiable PDF for regulatory purposes.

**Investors and Acquirers**

**Due diligence:** Before investing or acquiring, the investor verifies the Articles (to understand governance and share structure) and the Register of Members (to confirm the cap table). Discrepancies between what the company claims and what the registry holds are caught immediately.

**Legal Professionals**

**Litigation and disputes:** Solicitors verifying director authority, shareholder standing, or the company's constitutional provisions can clip and verify rather than relying on client-provided copies that may be outdated or altered.

**Insolvency practitioners:** When a company enters administration, the practitioner needs to confirm the current directors and shareholders. Verified register extracts provide certainty.

**Company Secretaries and Corporate Service Providers**

**Filing confirmation:** After filing amended Articles or a new director appointment, the company secretary can verify the filed version matches what was intended. This catches filing errors early.

**Counterparties in Commercial Transactions**

**Vendor and supplier onboarding:** Large companies onboarding new suppliers can verify the supplier's incorporation status, director register, and Articles as part of vendor qualification — faster and more reliable than requesting documents by email and reviewing them manually.

## 5. Trust Deed

When the ownership chain leads to a trust — as it frequently does for overseas clients of London fund managers — the Trust Deed is the constitutional document. It names the Settlor (who created the trust), the Trustees (who manage it), the Beneficiaries (who benefit from it), and the terms under which the trust operates. For KYC purposes, a fund manager needs to verify that the trust exists, that the people claiming to act for it are actually trustees, and that the beneficial ownership disclosed is consistent with the deed.

**Who issues the hash?** Trust deeds are private documents — no public registry holds the original in most jurisdictions (the UK's Trust Registration Service records the trust's existence but not the full deed text). The trust's professional trustee (a trust company like Meridian Trust Company (Jersey) Ltd) or the law firm that drafted the deed publishes the verification endpoint. The trust anchor is the professional trustee's domain, not a government registry.

**The Privacy Problem:** Trust deeds name beneficiaries, describe trust property, and contain sensitive family arrangements. The full text is hashed, but the verification response reveals only status — not content. A fund manager who receives the deed and clips it gets confirmation that the document is genuine and current. They do not need the verification endpoint to repeat the content back.

**Discretionary Trusts:** Many trusts have an open class of beneficiaries ("such other persons as the Trustees may appoint"). The deed names the initial beneficiaries, but the trustees can add more by letter of wishes or deed of appointment. Each such addition would be a separate verifiable document.

**How the law firm certification works:** The certification details — firm name, date, signatory — are **inside** the verifiable text block, not separate from it. The entire structure chart including the "Certified by" line is hashed as one unit, and that hash verifies against the law firm's domain (`mourant.com/kyc/v`). This means the law firm is the issuer of the whole document. If someone changes the diagram content, the hash breaks. If someone changes the certifier's name to a different firm, the hash breaks. If someone keeps the text identical but tries to verify it against a different domain, it fails because only Mourant's endpoint holds the hash.

The law firm doesn't just *sign* the structure chart — it *issues* it. The diagram and the certification are a single verifiable claim because the law firm authored both. This is the same pattern as a notarized document: the notary's statement is part of the document, not a detachable endorsement.

**The text representation problem:** Structure charts are typically visual diagrams — boxes and arrows in a PDF. For Live Verify, the chart must be rendered as text (as above) so that clip mode can hash it. The certifying firm produces the text version alongside or instead of the visual diagram. Camera mode could work with a printed text version.

**What it proves (and what it doesn't):** Verification confirms that Mourant Ozannes produced this specific structure chart on this date with this signatory. It does **not** independently prove each link in the chain — the BVI incorporation, the Jersey trust, the Jordanian UBO's identity. Those are separately verifiable if the underlying registries participate. The structure chart is the law firm's professional attestation that they have examined the underlying documents and this is the ownership structure they found. The fund manager can then verify the underlying documents individually for additional assurance.

**Why it matters for fund managers:** A London hedge fund receiving an investment from a BVI company owned by a Jersey trust settled by a Jordanian national needs to see the whole chain in one place. The structure chart is often the first document reviewed in KYC. If any link in the chain is fabricated — a company that doesn't exist, a trust that was never settled, a UBO who isn't who they claim to be — the structure chart verification fails because the certifying firm would not have issued the hash.

## 7. Who Can Certify: The Attestation Framework

When a London fund manager receives KYC documents from an overseas client, the documents themselves may be genuine but the fund manager has no direct relationship with the issuing registry. A Certificate of Incorporation from the BVI Companies Registry is meaningless if the fund manager cannot confirm it came from the registry.

The solution is **professional certification** — a trusted intermediary who examines the original documents and attests to their authenticity. The UK government and FCA generally accept certification from:

- **Notary Public** — the global standard. A notary's certification is recognized in virtually every jurisdiction. The notary examines the original, stamps the copy, and signs.
- **Lawyer or Solicitor** — registered with a national bar association or law society. The lawyer certifies they have seen the original and the copy is a true and complete reproduction.
- **Chartered Accountant** — a member of a recognized professional body (ICAEW, ACCA, ICAS, CPA Australia, etc.).
- **Embassy or Consular Official** — the client's country's embassy in London, or the British consulate in the client's country. Particularly useful for documents in non-English languages.

### FATF Grey List and Black List: Enhanced Due Diligence

If the client's country of origin is on the **FATF Grey List** (Jurisdictions under Increased Monitoring — as of 2026 this includes countries like Nigeria, South Africa, Turkey, and others) or the **FATF Black List** (High-Risk Jurisdictions subject to a Call for Action — currently Myanmar, Iran, North Korea), the standard certification may not be sufficient.

The fund manager may require a **Legal Opinion** from a law firm in the client's home jurisdiction, confirming:

1. The legality of the client's business activities under local law
2. The source of wealth is consistent with known business operations
3. There are no pending criminal, sanctions, or regulatory proceedings against the client or related entities
4. The corporate and trust structures comply with local law

## Verification Architecture

**The "Unverifiable PDF" Problem**

- **Forged certificates:** Convincing Certificate of Incorporation PDFs for companies that don't exist. Used in advance fee fraud, invoice fraud, and identity theft.
- **Altered Articles:** Articles with clauses removed, added, or modified — share classes changed, pre-emption rights deleted, voting thresholds lowered. The counterparty has no way to confirm the version they hold matches the filed version.
- **Stale registers:** A Register of Members showing ownership that was true six months ago but has since changed due to share transfers. Used in fraudulent share sales.
- **Ghost directors:** Register of Directors showing people who have resigned, or omitting people who have been appointed. Used to deny or claim authority.
- **Selective filing:** Company files amended Articles but provides counterparties with the old version. The old version is genuine — it was filed — but it's no longer current.

The verifiable claim addresses these because:

1. The registry issues the hash — documents are verified against the authority that holds the original
2. Amended documents cause the previous version to return SUPERSEDED — staleness is detectable
3. The "as at" date on register extracts makes the snapshot age explicit
4. Multi-page Merkle root hashing catches alterations to any page of the Articles
5. Each document type has its own verification endpoint — incorporation, articles, members, directors are separately verifiable

**Issuer Types** (First Party)

- **UK:** Companies House — Certificate of Incorporation, Articles of Association, Confirmation Statements, PSC register
- **US:** State Secretaries of State (Delaware Division of Corporations, California SOS, etc.) — Certificate of Incorporation, Articles of Incorporation. Bylaws are typically not filed with the state.
- **Germany:** Handelsregister (via Amtsgericht) — Handelsregisterauszug, Gesellschaftsvertrag (articles for GmbH)
- **France:** Greffe du Tribunal de Commerce — Extrait Kbis, Statuts (articles)
- **Ireland:** Companies Registration Office — Certificate of Incorporation, Constitution
- **Australia:** ASIC — Certificate of Registration, Constitution
- **Singapore:** ACRA — Certificate of Incorporation, Constitution
- **Hong Kong:** Companies Registry — Certificate of Incorporation, Articles of Association
- **Canada:** Corporations Canada / Provincial registries — Certificate of Incorporation, Articles, By-laws

## Broader Context: Bylaws and Unregistered Documents

In the US, corporate bylaws are not filed with the Secretary of State. They are internal governance documents maintained by the company. This creates a verification gap — a counterparty cannot check the bylaws against a registry because no registry holds them.

Two approaches address this:

1. **Law firm or registered agent verification:** The company's outside counsel or registered agent publishes the verified bylaws on their own domain. Verification is against the law firm's endpoint, not a government registry. The trust anchor is the law firm's reputation and professional obligations.

2. **Company self-publication:** The company publishes verified bylaws on its own domain. This is weaker — a company verifying its own documents is circular — but it still provides tamper detection. If someone alters the bylaws, the hash won't match.

In the UK and most Commonwealth jurisdictions, Articles of Association are filed with the registry, so this gap does not exist.

## Further Derivations

1. **Share Transfer Verification** — Individual share transfer instruments verified against the company's register, proving that a specific transfer was recorded and the register updated accordingly.
2. **Constitutional Amendment History** — A verifiable chain of all amendments to the Articles, allowing an investor to see not just the current version but the complete history of changes and when they occurred.
3. **Cross-Border Corporate Group Structures** — Verified formation documents and registers across multiple jurisdictions for corporate groups, allowing recursive verification of parent-subsidiary relationships (similar to the beneficial ownership chain in commercial-registry-filings).
4. **Trust Deed Amendments and Deeds of Appointment** — Verifiable amendments to trust deeds, deeds of retirement/appointment of trustees, and deeds of appointment adding beneficiaries to discretionary trusts.
5. **FATF-Correlated KYC Pack Verification** — A composite verification covering the entire KYC document bundle (incorporation, trust deed, registers, structure chart, certification, and legal opinion) as a single auditable event, with the fund manager's compliance team recording which documents were verified, when, and against which endpoints.
6. **Source of Funds vs. Source of Wealth** — Separate verification for "Source of Funds" (where did the money for this specific investment come from?) vs. "Source of Wealth" (how did the client accumulate their overall wealth?), each with its own attestation chain and legal opinion.
