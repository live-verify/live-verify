## What is a "What Is In It For You?" Disclosure?

Most conflict disclosures are too long, too vague, or too legalistic.

The customer usually wants a much simpler answer:

**What is in it for you if I say yes?**

This use case is the plain-English front sheet version of transaction-linked compensation disclosure. It is designed for ordinary people, in ordinary moments, making ordinary decisions about money, property, insurance, loans, investments, or services.

The point is not to teach the customer industry jargon. The point is to force a short, legible answer.

## Data Verified

In its simplest form, the object verifies only the fields a customer actually needs at the point of decision:

- whether any third-party commission exists
- whether any ongoing or trailing payment exists
- whether referral fees exist
- whether revenue share or equivalent incentive exists
- who pays
- what the customer pays directly
- a one-line summary in plain English

This is deliberately narrower than a full conflict disclosure or transaction compensation record.

## Data Visible After Verification

Shows the issuer domain and a simple status.

**Status Indications:**
- **Current** — The plain-English summary is current
- **Superseded** — A newer summary exists
- **Transaction Linked** — More detailed transaction record available
- **Expired** — Summary no longer current
- **Voided** — Summary issued in error

## Second-Party Use

The **customer** benefits most.

**Fast comparison:** Two advisers, two brokers, or two intermediaries can be compared in seconds without reading 30-page PDFs.

**Reduced social pressure:** Customers often feel awkward asking direct money questions in a meeting. A standard front sheet asks it for them.

**Complaint memory aid:** Months or years later, the customer can still show the simple summary they relied on when deciding.

## Third-Party Use

**Families and Trusted Contacts**

When someone vulnerable is being sold a product, a family member can quickly understand the incentive structure without specialist knowledge.

**Journalists, Ombudsmen, and Consumer Advocates**

The plain-English sheet makes it easier to test whether the short consumer-facing story matched the deeper legal disclosures behind it.

**Regulators**

Supervisors can require firms to provide not just technically correct disclosures, but intelligible ones.

## Verification Architecture

**The "Technically Disclosed, Practically Hidden" Problem**

- The incentive exists, but only in fine print.
- The customer receives a disclosure, but cannot realistically understand it.
- The adviser answers verbally in a softer way than the legal paperwork implies.
- Years later, the customer remembers "I thought they were fee-only" while the firm points to a buried paragraph on page 19.

This use case solves a narrower problem than the full transaction-linked disclosure:

not just whether the economics were legally documented,

but whether the customer got a short, legible answer to the question they actually cared about.

## Why This Is Especially Natural in UK Advice Markets

UK consumers have a longstanding cultural instinct to ask advisers whether they are genuinely independent and how they are paid.

This page turns that instinct into a standardized prompt:

**What is in it for you if I say yes?**

That question is short enough for a meeting room, a kitchen table, or a phone screen.

## Competition vs. Current Practice

| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |

| **Legally anchored** | **Yes.** | **Yes.** | **No.** |
| **Useful in complaints** | **High.** | **Medium.** | **Low.** |

**Practical conclusion:** full legal disclosures should remain, but they should be paired with a portable plain-English object that answers the customer's real question in one screen.
