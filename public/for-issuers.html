FOR ORGANIZATIONS THAT ISSUE DOCUMENTS

Your documents are being forged in your name.

Universities, regulators, insurers, certifiers, councils, hospitals: every certificate, licence, letter, and permit you issue can be reproduced perfectly by anyone with a chatbot and ten minutes. When the forgery surfaces, it carries your name — and today, nobody can check it without phoning you.

Small outfit — a trades firm, a single-site clinic, a parish council, a one-person training provider? This is for you too, and you don't need an engineering team. The rest of this page is written for larger institutions; if it's just you, skip it — the quickstart issues a real, revocable verifiable document on free hosting using a hosted hash tool, no code to write.

The cost you're already paying

  • A verification call centre you never wanted. Employers, landlords, embassies, and procurement teams email and phone you to ask "did you really issue this?" — a manual workload that scales with your success. Put a number on it: if one office fields 40 of these requests a week, that's a part-time salary spent confirming what a static file could confirm in four seconds, with no one on the phone. (Illustrative — but count your own inbox before you dismiss it.)
  • No way to revoke. A paper certificate you issued in 2019 to someone since struck off looks exactly as valid as one issued yesterday. Today you have no lever to pull; a live lookup lets you flip a genuine-but-no-longer-valid document to revoked everywhere, instantly.
  • Brand damage you don't control. When PPE Medpro supplied the NHS against a test report bearing Intertek's name — a report Intertek never issued — the forgery rode through goods-inwards across 72 shipments. The UK government established it was fake only much later, and that finding became a pivot of its lawsuit: courts ordered £122m repaid, but the goods were already landfill. The fake was checkable in principle from day one; there was just no way to check it.

What adopting looks like (smaller than you think)

You add one footer line to documents you issue:

This certifies that Sarah Chen was awarded MSc Computer Science on 28 June 2024. verify:degrees.youruniversity.ac.uk/c

And you publish a SHA-256 hash of each document's text at that URL on your own domain. Static file hosting is enough — a hash lookup is a plain HTTPS GET. No login system, no API platform, no blockchain, no per-verification fees, no vendor.

Anyone — an employer, a border officer, a procurement clerk — selects the text on screen or points a phone camera at the paper, and in about four seconds learns whether you stand behind it right now. Change the status file and it's revoked everywhere, instantly.

Effort estimate: a sprint, not a migration. The pipeline is text → normalize → SHA-256 → publish. Reference implementations, normalization rules, and cross-platform test fixtures are all Apache-2.0 on GitHub. A small engineering team can pilot with one document class in one to two weeks.

Your back catalog counts too

You don't need to wait for the next issuance cycle. Any record you already hold — degrees granted in 2020, licences still in force, certificates still active — can be made verifiable retrospectively: reconstruct the claim text from your system of record, hash it, publish. Start with the 100,000 records people keep asking you to verify, and the manual verification workload starts falling immediately.

What you are not signing up for

  • No PII leaves your systems. You publish one-way hashes, not document contents. The plaintext stays in your private systems; the public tier holds only hash → status.
  • No vendor, no fees, no lock-in. This is an open standard (Apache 2.0), like email or RSS. Adopt without permission; stop whenever you like — delete the files and you're out.
  • No change to how documents look. One footer line. No QR codes, special ink, NFC chips, or new hardware for you or for verifiers.
  • No central registry. Your hashes live on your domain, under your control and your jurisdiction. Nobody — including this project — sees who verifies what.

Be a founding issuer

The first ten pilot issuers get free advisory support from the standard's author — design review of canonical text templates, normalization fixtures, endpoint setup, and revocation workflow, by email, up to a sensible advice limit — in exchange for being nameable as an early adopter. Sustained implementation work is beyond the free scope and can be referred to commercial integration partners. Founding issuers are listed permanently on the founding adopters page.

Doesn't this depend on one person sticking around?

Reasonable question for anything you'd put in a footer for years. The answer is no, by construction: it's an open standard (Apache 2.0), publicly disclosed as unpatentable prior art, with no trademark to capture. Your verification endpoints are static files on your domain, not on anything this project runs — so even if the project stopped tomorrow, every document you've made verifiable stays verifiable, and anyone can carry the spec forward.
